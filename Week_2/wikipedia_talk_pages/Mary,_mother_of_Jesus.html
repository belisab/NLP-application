I don't know---I'm thinking it probably does just mean "a young woman" or similar:
  • HALOT, pp. 835-836 has it as "a marriageable girl", "a girl who is able to be married", and "a young woman (KBL: until the birth of her first child)";
  • The Dictionary of Classical Hebrew, Vol. 6 (ed. David Clines; pub. Sheffield Academic, 2011---couldn't find an online version, pardon!), p. 428, has it as "young woman—1. young woman, girl, perh. specif. uninitiated, unknowing female". (Note: I've excluded definition #2 only because it's just about music, heh.)
Those are perhaps the "gold standard" sources, but I tried checking around a bit more:
  • Strong's has it as "virgin, young woman (...of marriageable age; ...maid or newly married)" (OBU), "a lass (as veiled or private):— damsel, maid, virgin" (Strong), "young woman (ripe sexually; maid or newly married) / a girl of marriageable age" (BDB / Gesenius);
  • Klein's Comprehensive Etymological Dictionary of the Hebrew Language gives us "1. marriageable girl, maiden, young woman" & "2. miss";
  • Rashi on the Hamesh Megillot (Five Scrolls) has it (in re the pl.) as "young maidens [i.e.] virgins".
In none of these cases does it seem to have any sort of specific age attached; indeed, we read a fair diversity of opinion in your (final) link, wherein one commentator (Ibn Ezra) does propose the "younger than" definition... but the rest---and, seemingly, the Torah's own usage---disagree:
[...] consider why the Torah might refer to Rebecca as both a naarah and an almah. Ibn Ezra ... explains that the word almah denotes a girl who is younger than a naarah. [...] Peirush HaRokeach explains that the term almah refers to a girl who is less “outgoing” than the term naarah would indicate. Accordingly, Rebecca may have already reached the age of naarah and perhaps even advanced beyond that ... (if she was fourteen), yet she was still an almah [...] Rabbi Shimon Dov Ber Analak of Siedlce (1848-1907) explains that the two terms ... refer to two qualities characteristic of people in the age of adolescence. [...] Chizkuni ... contends that the words naarah and almah mean the exact same thing, but that naarah is a Hebrew word while almah is Aramaic.
Granted, "marriageable age" was undoubtedly much younger, for the Bronze Age Levant, than we'd be quite comfortable with---but still, the above militate against the idea that the word ever had any specific age attached: it seems clearly to mean a fairly broad range, and to be used perhaps with a particular connotation that suggests (but does not necessitate) innocence or chastity (which maybe implies "on the younger side of 'adolescent'", but again, I think, doesn't require it).
