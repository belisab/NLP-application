A fact from House of the Faun appeared on Wikipedia's Main Page in the Did you know column on 16 May 2007. The text of the entry was as follows:
  • Did you know... that the House of the Faun, one of the grandest private residences discovered in Pompeii, is named after the statue of a dancing faun (replica pictured), in its central impluvium?
A record of the entry may be seen at Wikipedia:Recent additions/2007/May.

Fauns with feet

[edit]

I'm familiar with the image of a faun as a creature with goatlike hooves and lower body, as described in the faun entry and in Wiktionary. To me this looks like a statue of a human being. Could someone explain the use of this term? Mike Serfas 13:20, 16 May 2007 (UTC)[reply]

I'm not entirley sure, but on the faun page, it is said that satyrs, which are closely connected to fauns, originally had human feet. Although this statue is a faun and not a satyr, it's possible the artist was inspired more by the satyr's appearance.--EAFEAF 19:01, 18 May 2007 (UTC)[reply]

The statue is indeed a satyr. Roman houses are typically named for unique objects or works of art found within them during excavations. The House of the Faun was named for the statue of the satyr that was erroneous identified as a faun.--Ewg118 (talk) 02:45, 9 April 2009 (UTC)[reply]

BC/AD vs BCE/CE

[edit]

@Materialscientist @107.115.41.103 Just wanted to throw my two cents in about the MOS and whether BC/AD or BCE/CE should be used, because looking through the history of the page I found that while the source code says that it has used BCE/CE since 2007, it has actually been mostly BC/AD since 2008 (first instance here) [1], with some changes back and forth. I'm not sure that one person saying "This article uses BCE/CE" 17 years ago overrides the majority of those 17 years where it has not been BCE/CE. Weirdguyz (talk) 05:00, 25 May 2025 (UTC)[reply]
