'Tanuki' is a valid term in English as a loanword from Japanese.

[edit]

'Raccoon Dog' is a poor name, they are not related to raccoons at all. By continuing to refer to them as 'Raccoon Dogs' just makes translators less accurate. 'Tanuki' as a loanword following English grammar rules, similar to 'Ninjas' and 'Samurais', makes 'Tanukis' also legitimate, 94.119.32.8 (talk) 00:04, 22 November 2023 (UTC)[reply]

Yeah, what's weirder is the fact that the word "Tanuki" isn't explained in the article, and isn't even mentioned until randomly in the folklore section. This was very confusing as a reader, as I hadn't realized that they were the same thing. Someone should definitely correct this, or atleast specify that it's also called a Tanuki. Blueyandicy (talk) 12:53, 2 December 2023 (UTC)[reply]
However, unlike "ninja" or "samuarai", "tanuki" is not recognised as a valid loanword in English. Oxford English Dictionary online has no entry on the term, whereas Merriam-Webster refers to it only as the folkloric totem and not as an alternative English common name for the species of animal. No descriptive writings of the animal in English reliable sources refer to it as tanuki, and the only English usage of the term are references to the Japanese folkloric totem character. Loopy30 (talk) 12:56, 10 April 2025 (UTC)[reply]
It is not really the job of a general-purpose dictionary such as the Oxford English Dictionary to determine whether the word tanuki is a valid English loanword or not. Many wild animal species, such as the culpeo, for exemple, also do not appear in such dictionaries, yet this does not invalidate their usage in scientific contexts. In this case, it is the field of zoology, not general lexicography, that is responsible for establishing such terminology. And in fact, the term tanuki is relatively common in scientific literature as an alternative name for Japanese raccoon dog, notably in publications associated with the American Society of Mammalogists.
What matters is whether the term tanuki refers only to the Japanese species, or to all members of the genus Nyctereutes. On that point, the vast majority of English-language sources clearly state that tanuki refers specifically to the Japanese species.
By that logic, the standalone term “tanuki” is recognized by the scientific community in English, whereas expressions like “Chinese tanuki” or “Korean tanuki”, when referring to certain subspecies of the common raccoon dog, are not considered officially valid. Futsuli (talk) 22:25, 16 July 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Yeah borrowed terms having their meaning slightly changed. Similar to how Taiko in English specifically refers to a Wadaiko, where in Japanese, Taiko literally just means (any) Drum.
Tanuki in English refers specifically to the Japanese Raccoon Dog, and in this English article, it should acknowledge them commonly being called Tanuki. 2A0A:EF40:5F5:DA01:832:1744:F116:AA20 (talk) 12:32, 31 August 2025 (UTC)[reply]
As a native English speaker, I've only ever heard or used the word "tanuki" to refer to these animals. I had never encountered the phrase "Japanese Raccoon Dog" until today, when I clicked to this article from a google search for "Tanuki". 64.72.94.4 (talk) 18:49, 23 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
That's my situation too! Barcodc (talk) 03:49, 30 November 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Same. I only know them as tanukis, I never called them Raccoon Dogs. A dictionary isn't going to determine common usage or loan words, especially if it isn't following trends. Common usage can also change, orca is now the more popular term, over killer whale and this is reflected in the article orca which used to be called killer whale. Before the 1980s, the few people in the West who knew about these animals would probably call them Japanese Raccoon Dogs, but with the advent of Super Mario Bros. 3, and greater knowledge of Japanese culture and folklore among Japanophiles, more people in the West now know them as tanukis. The article should probably be updated to reflect common usage of the present, Japanese Raccoon Dog is an outdated term. ~2025-43033-37 (talk) 15:32, 26 December 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Be careful, though. While the word 'tanuki' may have gained popularity through manga, video games, and anime, the term 'Japanese raccoon dog' remains the standard terminology in English scientific usage.
This likely stems from the same logic as 'African wild dog' for the Lycaon, or 'honey badger' for the ratel. These are compound terms that are easy to remember for non-native speakers—who make up the vast majority of people studying the Nyctereutes genus or canids in general.
As long as English remains a globalized language, such loanwords and shifts in naming are unlikely to happen as easily as they used to. Futsuli (talk) 16:47, 26 December 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Japanese Racoon Dog is not the scientific name of the animal. Nor does the title of the article use the scientific name necessarily, the title goes by most common usage in the English speaking world currently. ~2025-43033-37 (talk) 00:37, 31 December 2025 (UTC)[reply]
