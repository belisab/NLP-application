Animals evolved in the ocean and later colonised the land.

[edit]

Our article currently says

  • Animals originated in the ocean.

This claim is ambiguous -- originated can mean many things -- and it fails to introduce the paragraph which follows it. The rest of the paragraph concerns land colonization and cites sources which discuss evolution.

  • Rota-Stabelli, Omar; Daley, Allison C.; Pisani, Davide (2013). "Molecular Timetrees Reveal a Cambrian Colonization of Land and a New Scenario for Ecdysozoan Evolution". Current Biology. 23 (5): 392–398. Bibcode:2013CBio...23..392R. doi:10.1016/j.cub.2013.01.026. PMID 23375891. The title includes evolution and colonization.
  • Clack, Jennifer A. (21 November 2005). "Getting a Leg Up on Land". Scientific American. 293 (6): 100–107. Bibcode:2005SciAm.293f.100C. doi:10.1038/scientificamerican1205-100. PMID 16323697. First sentence is "Recent fossil discoveries cast light on the evolution of four-limbed animals from fish".

I propose to change the sentence to

  • Animals evolved in the ocean and later colonised the land.

Johnjbarton (talk) 16:21, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]

@Johnjbarton Evolving and originating mean completely different things. You are proposing to substitute the sentence "Animals originated in the ocean" (meaning that the ancestors of all animals appeared in the ocean, which is correct) with "Animals evolved in the ocean and later colonised the land" (meaning that at some point they stopped evolving in the ocean, which is incorrect).
Animals are always evolving, as are all living organisms, and most animal groups continue to evolve in the ocean right now. Not all animals "colonised the land", and both references that you have provided are perfect examples of this.
Four-limbed animals (aka tetrapods) are only one of the few lineages of animals that began evolving to transition into land habitats, and despite that, many have transitioned back into oceans (see whales). Among ecdysozoans, several lineages transitioned into land independently, but others remain in the oceans (like crabs).
Your proposed new sentence is the equivalent of saying "humans evolved in Africa and later colonised Australia". Sounds correct at first glance, but is very inaccurate. — Snoteleks (talk) 17:08, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
@Snoteleks So lets's try "Animals initially evolved in the ocean and later some species colonized land". This way we get the to key words that the paragraph is concerned with, evolution and colonization, in the first introductory sentence. This would be comparable to "humans first evolved in Africa and some humans later colonised Australia" which would both correct and adequately correct for an introductory sentence about colonizing Australia. Johnjbarton (talk) 17:36, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
@Johnjbarton That is already what the same paragraph explains later ("Animals originated in the ocean [...] several lineages of arthropods begun to colonise land around the same time as land plants [...] vertebrates such as the lobe-finned fish Tiktaalik started to move on to land in the late Devonian [...] Animals occupy virtually all of earth's habitats and microhabitats, with faunas adapted to salt water, hydrothermal vents, fresh water, hot springs, swamps, forests, pastures, deserts, air, and the interiors of other organisms"). You would only be adding an overly simplified sentence that confuses readers. "Originate" is the adequate verb to use here. — Snoteleks (talk) 18:16, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
I continue to disagree and I hope other editors will weigh in. "Originated" is unnecessarily ambiguous. Johnjbarton (talk) 18:31, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
How is it ambiguous? The origin of animals occurred in the ocean, once. They never stopped evolving in the ocean, they are constantly evolving in the ocean as anywhere else. — Snoteleks (talk) 20:34, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Originated could be spontaneous or divine. "Animals initially evolved in the ocean" is specific. I grant that my first version, "Animals evolved in the ocean" could be read as "Animals continually evolved in the ocean". Maybe we just need to get rid of the sentence. Johnjbarton (talk) 22:34, 15 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
I agree that Animals evolved in the ocean and later colonised the land. reads as if animals evolved in the ocean and that these evolved animals later colonised the land. I don't have a particular problem with "originated", but perhaps something like the first/earliest animals evolved in the ocean would be better.  —  Jts1882 | talk  08:31, 16 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]

I do not understand what is the possible ambiguity with the verb "originated". It seems to me the current version I proposed (diff) is quite clear in postponing and clarifying the colonization of land. --cyclopiaspeak! 15:46, 16 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]

Evolutionary origin

[edit]

The current content in "Evolutionary origin" does not match reliable secondary sources. Specifically our content makes the evidence sound much more certain by citing primary sources. For example, we say

  • " the discovery of the animal lipid cholesterol in fossils of Dickinsonia establishes their nature"

citing

  • Bobrovskiy, I., Hope, J. M., Ivantsov, A., Nettersheim, B. J., Hallmann, C., & Brocks, J. J. (2018). Ancient steroids establish the Ediacaran fossil Dickinsonia as one of the earliest animals. Science, 361(6408), 1246-1249.

but a review like

  • Dos Reis, M., Thawornwattana, Y., Angelis, K., Telford, M. J., Donoghue, P. C., & Yang, Z. (2015). Uncertainty in the timing of origin of animals and the limits of precision in molecular timescales. Current biology, 25(22), 2939-2950.

emphasize the many complex issues including the claims of the primary source above. I think our content should reflect the continuing uncertainty. Johnjbarton (talk) 22:11, 13 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]

The section also jumps back and forth in a confusing mix of time and certainty. The focus of the content is "when" but the section name would suggest more about key common ancestors. To me these are both valid topics so perhaps we need two subsections "Earliest examples" and "Biological ancestors". Johnjbarton (talk) 22:22, 13 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Before I dive in the sources, I would like to understand your argument. How can a 2015 review emphasize issues about a 2018 research? cyclopiaspeak! 08:55, 14 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]
Primary sources generally seek to present their result in the most positive light. Reviews, by their nature, take into consideration much broader points of view. Our statement about the Bobrovskiy work makes it appear to be a definitive breakthrough.
  • Wood, R., Liu, A. G., Bowyer, F., Wilby, P. R., Dunn, F. S., Kenchington, C. G., ... & Penny, A. (2019). Integrated records of environmental change and evolution challenge the Cambrian Explosion. Nature ecology & evolution, 3(4), 528-538.
mentions Bobrovskiy in passing, saying:
The first identifiable motile, heterotrophic organisms are Kimberella quadrata, Yorgia and Dickinsonia from Russia, at ~558–555 Ma, the latter being highly likely to have been a metazoan on the basis of ichnological, developmental and biomarker evidence
  • Yang, C., Rooney, A. D., Condon, D. J., Li, X. H., Grazhdankin, D. V., Bowyer, F. T., ... & Zhu, M. (2021). The tempo of Ediacaran evolution. Science advances, 7(45), eabi9643.
says:
  • The first appearance datum of Dickinsonia, together with the oldest metazoan lipid biomarkers (31), is ca. 90 m below the 557.3 ± 0.6–Ma ash bed in the White Sea area.
  • Kaçar, B. (2024). Reconstructing early microbial life. Annual Review of Microbiology, 78(2024), 463-492.
says
  • Fossilized lipids preserved in sedimentary rocks can offer insights into ecological trends across high-level clades when unique lipid synthesis pathways can be mapped onto distinct extant organisms.
and I think there are more. Our presentation of the the Bobrovskiy work is inconsistent with secondary sources.
Perhaps there are newer secondary sources that back up our claim of certainty, but my understanding is more like there is a lot of evidence pointing to the Ediacaran timeline. Johnjbarton (talk) 16:37, 14 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]
