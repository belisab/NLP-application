Oldest rocks

[edit]

An edit from April 26th of this year correctly cites some research on zircon crystals from 4400 million years ago. But attributing that age to existing "rock beds" (identified as a little older than 3800 Ma in the prior edit) is somewhat misleading, because the zircons are detrital -- they're grains that eroded out of pre-existing rocks. Those older rocks no longer exist, only the zircons do, so the oldest existing rock beds still only date to around 3800 Ma. I'll clarify this in the paragraph about the Hadean Eon. Cephal-odd 17:03, 26 December 2006 (UTC)[reply]

supereon?

[edit]

Should we call the Precambrian a "supereon"? The term may be out there, but doesn't look like many stratigraphers use it to refer to the Precambrian. It's more common to speak informally of "Precambrian time". Moreover, some geologists have suggested a Geozoic supereon that overlaps considerably with the Precambrian. Preceding unsigned comment added by Cephal-odd (talk contribs) 17:38, 12 September 2009 (UTC)[reply]

I recommend amending "supereon" to "Eon". "Supereon" does not appear as a level in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart. A search of Episodes (the official IUGS journal) shows no publications using this term in an abstract or title. The current terminology is "Eonothem" (chronostratigraphic unit) or "Eon" (geochronological unit). There are obvious difficulties in the Chart (multiple levels are included under the Precambrian Eon), but supereons are not currently included in official terminology.Te Karere (talk) 07:17, 23 June 2018 (UTC)[reply]
The point about the Geozoic Supereon is that it identifies the period where there is life on earth; as Kowalewski et al. say:"The Geozoic denotes the time of lifes existence on our planet" (The Genozoic Supereon; PALAIOS, 2011, v. 26, p. 251255, DOI: 10.2110/palo.2011.S03) It has since been found in a number of related papers, it is finding its way into books, and seems not to be related to specific Geologic identifiers. I think it would be confusing for it to be used for such, as in calling the Precambrian a supereon. It should also be noted that it was coined because the term Phanerozoic (visible life) was no longer entirely useful, because there is clear evidence of life that goes well back into the Precambrian. Earnulf Gery (talk) 12:21, 25 September 2022 (UTC)[reply]

eon(s)
Precambrian EON is comprised by THREE other EONs ? (Hadean, Archaean and Proterozoic EONS)
Next EON is the Phanerozoic, but it is comprised of three ERAs (Paleozoic, Mesozoic and Cenozoic ERAs)
So, like an Eon is comprised of Eras and an Era is comprised of Periods... and Periods are comprised of Epochs...

I have removed the word 'period' after one instance of 'Precambrian' in this article. The Precambrian is also presented elsewhere in the article (and rightly, I would assert) as a 'supereon' and it is then stated to be divided into various eons. The accompanying geologic table has it as an 'eon'. The term 'Precambrian' is an awkward one for which a single appropriate word denoting 'interval of geologic(al) time' is hard to find and which, as a result is abused. It is, so far as I'm aware, the sole example of a 'supereon' but is more often referred to as a 'period' (or indeed an 'eon') - both these terms being used in the non-technical sense. This article more than any other ought to address this issue.
I have edited many Wikipedia pages replacing 'era' with period' when referring to the Jurassic, Cambrian etc etc and sometimes replacing 'period' with 'era' when referring to the Mesozoic etc but when it comes to Precambrian, it is difficult to know what to do for the best. I have tried supereon (with the wikilinking square brackets to provide the uninitiated with an idea as to what a supereon is) but often the popular (but technically inaccurate) words 'period or 'era' seem to fit better (stylistically at least) and I have guiltily let them be. Comment welcome! cheers Geopersona (talk) 07:10, 12 December 2010 (UTC)[reply]

What percentage of geologic(al) time does the Precambrian represent?

[edit]

If we're settled on the Cambrian starting 542 million years ago then we need to ask when we consider geologic(al) time to have started. The article Age of the Earth would suggest we go for 4567 million years ago. That leaves us with a Precambrian supereon extending over some 4025 million years. If we do the math(s) then that gives us figure of 88.1% of all of geologic(al) time being constituted by the Precambrian - neither the 85% nor the 87% figure which have appeared on this page recently. Anyone care to offer up a different form of reasoning? thanks Geopersona (talk) 20:04, 20 December 2010 (UTC)[reply]

A minor update: the Age of Earth article now offers up 4540 million years and the start of the Cambrian has recently been altered to 538.8 ma so the Precambrian 'supereon' on current figures amounts to 4001.2 million years - the (math)s then offers up a figure of 88.13% of all of geologic(al) time being constituted by the Precambrian. Expressed in vulgar fractions, that's marginally closer to 7/8 (87.5%) than it is to 8/9 (88.89%). For simplicity, I've neglected the uncertainties which attend both dates. Geopersona (talk) 12:48, 15 October 2022 (UTC)[reply]

Misleading graphic

[edit]

It is unfortunate that this article now sports (at top right) an outwardly attractive graphic image titled 'Precambrian Period' and displaying a palaeoglobe as it does. Firstly the Precambrian time interval is not a 'period' in the ordinarily understood sense of the term used by geologists - at least we might put 'period' into inverted commas or else dispense with the term entirely. Secondly there is no one palaeogeographical layout which makes any sense at all for a time interval which represents nearly 9/10 of the Earth's history and for a part of which there were no differentiated regions of continental and oceanic crust. Anyone else care to comment? cheers Geopersona (talk) 19:36, 23 June 2013 (UTC)[reply]

Removed the Geological period template due to inappropriate "period" bit. Retained the image with a relevant caption. Vsmith (talk) 21:50, 23 June 2013 (UTC)[reply]

Beginning of Precambrian in infobox

[edit]

The lead says the Precambrian began about 4.6 billian years ago. The Overview section says the Earth coalesced at roughly 4,543 Ma. The infobox gives the beginning date as 4567.3 +_ 0.16 Ma. That very precise date is taken from [1], which also has a chart displaying the date in a rounded form, 4567 Ma. Supereon and Supereonothem are not defined, even provisionally

[edit]

I'm putting this here, although I'm not sure if it's a problem with this article, Template:Infobox geologic timespan, or the article Geologic Time Scale.

This page's instance of the template uses the value "supereon" for the parameter "chrono_unit" and the value "supereonothem" for the parameter "strat_unit". The template makes both of these into links to Geologic Time Scale#Terminology. However there is no definition for "supereon" or "supereonothem" provided on that page. The former appears just once, only to link back here, and the latter doesn't appear at all.

I see there's been discussion here in the past regarding the validity of the term "supereon", but that's only indirectly connected to my issue. My issue is that a link is conceptually a quod vide: a pointer to further information. If a page X says page Y contains further information, then page Y must actually have that information. If not, the link should be changed to point somewhere that does. And if there is no further information available, then the link should not exist. Failing to do so feels like a small betrayal of trust between Wikipedia and the user.

Either:

  • in this article, the values of the parameters "chrono_unit" and "strat_unit" should be changed, or
  • Template:Infobox geologic timespan should be modified to not form links for "supereon" or "supereonothem", or
  • Template:Infobox geologic timespan should be modified to link them somewhere relevant, or
  • definitions of "supereon" and "supereonothem" should be added to the article Geologic Time Scale, including the caveat that they are provisional and informal.

19:35, 12 September 2025 (UTC) 2600:4040:A05F:B200:88FF:A2E6:F128:9815 (talk) 19:35, 12 September 2025 (UTC)[reply]
