Muscle fibers types concentration and muscle metabolism in cheetahs.

[edit]

Here is a compilation of the concentration of fast-twitch glycolytic muscle fibres, or type IIx, in cheetahs and other athletic animals, compared to humans.

This concentration will be shown as a percentage and will be seen for the vastus lateralis muscle.


-Wild cheetahs: 76%.

-Captive cheetahs: 62%.

-Wild springboks: 58%.

-Wild mountain reedbucks: 57%.

-Wild blesboks: 55%.

-Wild European fallow deer: 48%.

-Wild greater kudus: 43%.

-Wild black wildebeests: 30%.

-Recreationally active humans: 7%.


As an extra, I will also show the concentration of fast-twitch oxidative-glycolytic muscle fibres, or type IIa, in domestic animals known for their athleticism.

(Type IIa fibres have a lower capacity for using glycolysis without oxygen and creatine phosphate, and therefore less powerful contractions than type IIx fibres, but their greater use of oxygen gives them more resistance to fatigue).


-Racing greyhounds: 76%.

-Racehorses: 61% (although this is the percentage for the vastus medialis muscle).


I will also show the level of activity of the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) in the muscles of these animals compared to that of human muscles, an indicator of the muscle's greater capacity to use glycolytic and anaerobic energy sources (glycogen and creatine phosphate) as fuel, and a lesser capacity to use oxygen.


-LDH activity is nine times higher in wild cheetahs than in humans (and 2.8 times higher than in racehorses, although LDH activity in horses was observed in the vastus medialis).


-The activity of this enzyme is six times higher in captive cheetahs than in humans.


-LDH activity is four times higher in wild antelopes than in humans.

(Differences in type IIx fibers concentration between species possibly due to differences in body mass).


-LDH activity is three times higher in greyhounds than in humans.


These two muscular characteristics in cheetahs indicate muscular adaptations for high-intensity, short-duration physical exertion (anaerobic muscular activities) and help explain the speed of this species.

UPDATE:
I found comparative data for cheetahs and other animals concerning to type IIx muscle fibers concentration for overall musculature of the hind limbs, neck, and trunk (including extrinsic muscles of the forelimbs and abdominals), and for the forelimbs (only the arm and forearm).
Here is the concentration of fast-twitch glycolytic or type IIx muscle fibres in cheetahs and other ‘powerful’ animals (so to speak).
•Cheetahs:
-Hindlimbs: 50.1%.
-Neck and trunk: 40%.
-Forelimbs: 36%.
•Thoroughbred horses:
-Hindlimbs: 19.5%.
-Trunk: 16%.
-Abdominals: 2.8%.
-Head and neck: 3.7%.
-Forelimbs: 7%.
•African savannah elephant:
-Hindlimbs: 11.4%.
-Neck and trunk: 11.4%.
-Forelimbs: 20.8%.
(Note: Domestic cats also appear to have a similar concentration of type IIx fibers than that of cheetahs, so the difference in locomotor performance between these two animals may be due to the difference in muscle mass in the hind limbs, with cheetahs having more locomotor muscle than cats but maintaining a similar concentration of type IIx fibers; the longer length of the limbs in relation to the body mass in cheetahs may be another determining factor).

Cheetah's tail: Counterbalance or rudder?

[edit]

Contrary to what is often said, cheetah tails most likely do not function as a counterbalance.

Anatomical studies of cheetah carcasses have found that cheetah tails represent only ~2% of their body mass.

(For a tail to serve as an effective counterweight in an animal, it must represent at least 10% of its body mass).

Furthermore, the distance between the centre of mass of the tail and its base represents ~25% of its total length.

In other words, the centre of mass of the tail is close to its base and not in the middle or near the tip.

Apart from the fact that the main muscles that move the tail are also around the base of the tail, the tail itself is mostly made up of bone, tendons, ligaments, skin and hair (the latter two represent about 25% of the total mass of the tail).

Therefore, the cheetah's tail does not provide significant inertia to serve as a counterbalance.


What has been found is that the tail can generate greater aerodynamic drag.

The thick hair on the cheetah's tail increases its effective area by 40% without having to increase its muscle and bone mass, which would make the tail too heavy and compromise the animal's athletic ability.

A 24 cm cylinder with a diameter of 30 mm at one end and 10 mm at the other was covered with a 29 mm layer of cheetah tail fur.

Its effective diameter was as if it had a diameter of 60 mm, assuming a cylinder with equal diameters at both ends.

The tail reduces the force load on the front limbs and increases it on the hind limbs.

This means that the cheetah's tail serves more as a rudder to control the animal's direction (and a rudder with a larger area improves manoeuvrability).


In addition, it has been found that cheetah tails perform rotational movements that form an imaginary cone shape in the air.

These rotational movements of the tail could reach angular velocities of 17–22 rads/s (162–210 revolutions per minute) and angles of 90°.

This could help stabilise the animal when running at high speeds.
