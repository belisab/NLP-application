Domestication vs. slavery

[edit]

I am quite revolted by the explanations on this page. Form my point of view, domestication includes the willingness of the domesticated to a participate to the relationship. Also, domestication implies for the most part, safety between the two parties, the domesticated and the domesticator. (The exception is the sacrifice of the domesticated beings, e.g. for food.)

Yes, a slave has the net benefit of being allowed to live, as long as it / he obeys satisfactorily. But is the slave "domesticated"? Are the circus animals domesticated? Lions, tigers... After all, they provide work, entertaining us - it fits the definition provided on the page perfectly.

The same discussion about the domestication of plants. We cannot make any assessment on their willingness to be our partners. We use them in any way we want and they have no ability to defend themselves. How an we guarantee that their actual status is not "slavery"? I think that it is safe to to say that there are 3 states with regard to domestication: domesticated, wild, and other / unknown / undefinable. And plants definitely go to the 3rd category. Possibly the silk worms too. I am curious if the beer yeast is domesticated, according to the definitions on the page.

So while the intention of the page is good, the actual result is quite terrible, I would say. And unfortunately, I am not sure how it can be improved professionally. Virolino (talk) 13:02, 15 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]

Domestication of Chickens

[edit]

On mobile so edits are a challenge. Suffice it to say, the idea that chickens were originally domesticated for cockfighting comes from a misreading of the anecdote in the (current) source article. A better source is here: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2121978119

The “purpose” is unknowable but likely was paired to rice cultivation as an additional source of food calories.


This article also talks about other purposes for chickens that contributed to their spread: https://www.science.org/content/article/how-wild-jungle-fowl-became-chicken 217.141.114.38 (talk) 19:50, 21 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]

Good find. We can't use Science.org but I've used the PNAS source to rewrite the mention of chicken domestication. Will do that article as well. Chiswick Chap (talk) 07:19, 22 October 2025 (UTC)[reply]
