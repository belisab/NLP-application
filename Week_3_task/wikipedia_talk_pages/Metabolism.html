Benzene

[edit]

I've been keeping an eye on Metabolism lately, and I see the latest reverted edit may have some significant information. An IP, 24.154.111.112, added the following to the TOP of the article:

Benzene as it applies to Metabolism on a cellular level is not an organic solvent. It is however, on a microscopic scale, a quantum coupling agent. There is a very large difference between organic solvents and quantum coupling agents on a microscopic scale.

Now, while the position of this text at the top of the page was non-standard, the material itself might be worthy of attention. The only mention of Benzene in the article is the following:

Key biochemicals → Lipids

[...] Lipids are usually defined as hydrophobic or amphipathic biological molecules that will dissolve in organic solvents such as benzene or chloroform. [...]

So, does it mislead readers who will infer that benzene is used by the body to dissolve lipids? I've read that Benzene is metabolized mostly in the liver and very quickly. Its byproducts are used by our cells to produce more energy. If, as the IP suggests, Benzene is not used to dissolve lipids, then this article should not tell readers that it does, isn't that so? I'm not an expert and will gladly defer to those who are. – Paine Ellsworth CLIMAX! 08:38, 2 October 2013 (UTC)[reply]

That edit sounds like gibberish to me, I don't think benzene is a normal part of any organism's metabolism. It is one of many foreign substances that can be degraded by xenobiotic metabolism/ Tim Vickers (talk) 20:10, 9 November 2021 (UTC)[reply]

Opinions needed, does source support articles claims?

[edit]

Our article claims: This proved that the organic compounds and chemical reactions found in cells were no different in principle than any other part of chemistry., which is currently uncited. I believe I have found the source for this but I am wondering if the source is truly saying this (chem isn't my strong suit)

per page 292 and 294 of this source [1]: "This similarity (of the product obtained) with urea prompted me to perform comparative experiments with pure urea isolated from urine which demonstrated unambiguously that urea of urine and the former crystallized matter or the ammonium cyanate, if it could be called that way, are completely identical compounds." and "If, however, by combining cyanic acid and ammonia really only urea is generated, urea must have the same composition (of elements) as that obtained when calculating it from the formula of cyanic acid salts; which, indeed, is the case..." and "Thus, without demonstrating the generation of urea from cyanic acid and ammonia experimentally, one could have also calculated in advance that ammonium cyanate together with one atom of water has the same composition as urea." and finally "that he kind of cannot hold his chemical water if he wouldn’t inform him about the fact that he (Wöhler) is capable of producing urea requiring neither kidneys nor any animal, may it be man or dog. Ammonium cyanate is urea ...and it is by no hemical means different from urea of the urine, which I have produced all by myself" (direct quotes are used in the pub so I'm assuming it's okay to use them here as well). This seems to be supporting what we have written but I am unsure. IntentionallyDense (Contribs) 20:20, 31 March 2025 (UTC)[reply]

Hi! With the caveat that chemistry AND history are not my strong suit and I usually do not edit the history sections, my only thought is that given this source only discusses urea, it may be a stretch to apply this as a 'proven concept' for all chemical reactions found in cells. If this researcher only proved this with urea, can we change the text slightly to reflect this? Sorry, I am not even certain if this is helpful! You did an amazing job tackling this! JenOttawa (talk) 04:21, 1 April 2025 (UTC)[reply]
The usual way this topic is addressed in textbooks is with a different logic. Our current statement is a broad theory one about the equivalence of whatever happens in cells vs what happens out of cells. As you note, this experiment does not prove its universal applicability. Instead, this experiment is one that is often highlighted as helping disprove the broad theory of difference between whatever happens in cells is different from what happens out of cells. All it really takes is one counter-example. DMacks (talk) 05:15, 1 April 2025 (UTC)[reply]
I agree with your statement of "it's a bit of stretch" and that was what I was having a hard time identifying. Jared's wording looks good and I think is a much better representation of what the source says. IntentionallyDense (Contribs) 03:48, 2 April 2025 (UTC)[reply]
