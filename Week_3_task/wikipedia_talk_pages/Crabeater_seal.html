Just curious, do these animals actually eat crabs?

[edit]

It sounds like they don't, so why the moniker. This would be something interesting to add to the article, I bet.

Krill is in the biological world no crab, but the name is traditional Uwe Kils 19:02, July 18, 2005 (UTC)

  • The folks who coined the current name didn't mean crabs: Il se nourrit principalement de crevettes, ce qui donne une coloration rouge à ses excréments. C'est cette circonstance des mœurs de cette espèce qui a déterminé nos voyageurs à lui donner la dénomination qu'elle porte "It feeds mainly on shrimp, which gives a red coloration to its feces. It is this circumstance of the customs of this species which determined our travelers to give it the name it bears". Even though from the modern point of view krill is no shrimp either (the former has a head separate from the carapace, the latter has them fused together; the discoverers probably hadn't investigated the animal's diet properly), "crab-eater" is a mistranslation originating already in the 1840s. Ain92 (talk) 14:36, 19 August 2022 (UTC) PS I actually suggest that this historical aspect is covered in the article.[reply]

Commonness as a 'large creature'

[edit]

How large is "large"?

There are several billion humans, and tens of millions of dogs in the United States alone. Depending on where one draws the line between dogs that one considers large creatures and those that one doesn't, the number of 'large dogs' must be well over 15 million.

Aren't there lots of horses and cattle, too? Wildebeest?

A safe qualification would be to describe them as the most common wild, predatory animal larger than humans. Nobody in his right mind would breed dogs larger than an adult human because a dog that size would be too dangerous to have nearby.

There are at least 1.3 billion cattle so this is definitely not true.

carcinophagus

[edit]

Since "lobodon" is a masculine noun (https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Lobodon), the adjective should be also masculine "carcinophagus" (https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/carcinophagus#Latin). <- This wiktionary entry also includes "lobodon carcinophagus". If you search Google Scholar for "lobodon carcinophagus", you get about 2460 hits, whereas you get about 1030 for "lobodon carcinophaga". So, I guess that "lobodon carcinophagus" should be the international official species name on the first line of the main article and then it can mention that "lobodon carcinophaga" is used synonymously. Ryofurue (talk) 08:09, 13 February 2025 (UTC)[reply]

You are technically correct, which in this case is the worst kind of correct. Take a look at the usage in the links in the taxonbar. Overwhelmingly they use carcinophaga. - UtherSRG (talk) 12:23, 13 February 2025 (UTC)[reply]
