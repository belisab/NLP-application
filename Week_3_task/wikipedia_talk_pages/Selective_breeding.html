Artificial selection and selective breeding are not the same thing (for @Chiswick Chap:)

[edit]

All selective breeding is artificial selection, but not all artificial selection is selective breeding.

The simplest way to see this is that selectively culling animals is obviously also a form of artificial selection that can cause evolution (i.e., it changes the frequency of the trait in the population, and if the trait is heritable, it will result in a change in allele frequencies over time), but selective culling is clearly not selective breeding. Selective culling is artificial viability selection, and selective breeding is artificial fertility selection.

Further, selective breeding is intentional, whereas artificial selection is not necessarily intentional (e.g., [1]). Reminder: Darwin is the person who coined both "natural selection" and "artificial selection", and he defined two kinds of artificial selection: "methodical [intentional] selection", which would include selective breeding, and "unconscious [unintentional] selection", which would include things like widespread antibiotic use selecting for resistant variants. (See [2]) Darwin's key argument in Origin was more or less "We all agree that intentional artificial selection can cause changes over generations, but so can unintentional artificial selection. And if you accept that human intent is not required, then you should accept that nature itself can do the selecting: and that's natural selection."

So, these two pages probably should not have been merged. If these two pages are to be merged, then the page should be for the umbrella term, "artificial selection", not for the subset of artificial selection called "selective breeding". Bueller 007 (talk) 01:55, 23 May 2025 (UTC)[reply]
