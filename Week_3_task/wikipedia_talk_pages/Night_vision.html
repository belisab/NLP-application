amplification in image intensifiers

[edit]

The section on image intensifiers states that they do not 'amplify' I *believe* that they in fact do, just not in the commonly percieved way; an electronic signal amplifier, such as found in a stereo. My understanding is that the image intensifier unit is a specialized photomultiplier, and the effect is in fact 'amplification'. Once I've verified this, I'll be editing to this effect, that they do in fact 'amplify', but not through the use of amplifier circuits, but photomultipliers sensitive to IR. —Preceding unsigned comment added by 68.116.67.106 (talk) 07:07, 16 December 2007 (UTC)[reply]

Removing WWII poster

[edit]

Because the premise of this poster is intentionally misleading, it is inappropriate for an article on human night vision.

This poster was part of a disinformation campaign to prevent the Germans from learning of British use of radar in World War II. The story was that British pilots ate many carrots to improve their night vision, but this was untrue. They were actually being guided by radar.

Smithsonian Magazine: A WWII Propaganda Campaign Popularized the Myth That Carrots Help You See in the Dark.

—Ryan (talk) 13:17, 20 February 2016 (UTC)[reply]

This is a good biology article.

[edit]

This is how it should be! A biology article that actually has loads of animal coverage? This is super rare!

90% of biology articles exclusively talk about human biology unless that article is specifically about animals or is about something we don't have (this doesn't count because we HAVE night vision our night vision is just bad)

Great job people. LoverOfAllAnimalsActivist (talk) 12:02, 5 February 2024 (UTC)[reply]

Hi @LoverOfAllAnimalsActivist and welcome to Wikipedia editing! I see that you have a lot of strong opinions about bio and anatomy articles being human-centric, and I mostly agree. I posted recently here for my own sake, not to complain about the human centricity so much, but looking for some guidance on how to handle it orginizationally. There were some good references posted in reply. Its important to remember that when a reader looks up vomiting for example, that they are generally wanting to read information about human vomiting. Combined with the fact that we know much more about human vomiting and that there are far more experts on human vomiting, its no wonder that the articles will organically tend to be human centric. That's not to say it has to stay that way:
  • In articles where non-human content is in the minority and easily separable, it is generally put in an "other animals section", like in visible spectrum or as you started in floater.
  • In articles where comparative biology is important, there is generally an even split between human and non-humans like in color vision, and each main section should "ideally" be species-ambiguous, as far as is reader-friendly.
  • In articles where there is sufficient human and non-human information, but it is not useful to mix the information together, the articles are generally split, like in eye and human eye.
  • In articles where humans do not really present a special case, the article can be framed in a way to focus only on the super-group, hence why I recently redirected evolution of human colour vision to evolution of color vision in primates, since there is nothing special to say about humans specifically that doesn't apply to all apes.
  • Then there is also color blindness, where any reference to non-human animals was removed since animal color vision should not be judged in comparison to human color vision.
All different ways of handling the balance between human and non-human info. This balance will of course change as more non-human info is added to an article, and sometimes an article needs to be rewritten to better de-humanize (balance) it, so to speak. Not much to do until that info accumulates in an "other animals" section though. The only real problem is when human-centrists start removing animal information, which was apparently a problem in several anatomy articles recently, like pelvis. Its not an easy thing to balance on wikipedia, so please don't think that the status quo is somehow "ridiculous" or "preposterous". Most of us are just working to create an article that gives the reader the information they are looking for. Curran919 (talk) 19:58, 5 February 2024 (UTC)[reply]
